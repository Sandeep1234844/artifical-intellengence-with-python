1.     would you say that Deep Blue was intelligent? Explain.

Ans. Deep Blue was intelligent in a narrow, mechanical sense it could perform intelligent-looking behavior in chess. But it lacked the broader traits of true or general intelligence, such as understanding, learning, creativity, or reasoning beyond its programming.

2.    what contributed to Deep Blue's success and how? Who/what should get credit for it?

1. Massive Computational Power

Deep Blue could analyze about 200 million chess positions per second.

This allowed it to explore far more possibilities than any human could in the same time.

Its raw computing speed was one of the main reasons it could outplay Garry Kasparov.

2. Sophisticated Algorithms

It used search algorithms like minimax with alpha-beta pruning to eliminate poor moves quickly.

These algorithms helped it focus on the most promising moves instead of wasting time on every possible option.

The system was also able to evaluate positions using complex evaluation functions, which mimicked expert reasoning about chess positions.

3.     3. Human Expertise

Chess grandmasters and computer scientists worked together to design and tune Deep Blues evaluation functions.

Human experts helped program strategic insights such as recognizing piece activity, king safety, and control of the center.

In other words, Deep Blues intelligence came largely from human-designed knowledge.

4.     4. IBMs Engineering Team

The team of IBM engineers and researchers who built and optimized the hardware and software deserve much of the credit.

They not only created the algorithms but also designed custom hardware specifically for chess computations.

5. Preparation and Data

Deep Blues team studied Kasparovs past games and prepared strategies that exploited his weaknesses just like human competitors do.

This preparation gave the machine a psychological and tactical edge.

9. How is the Turing Test conducted?

In the test, a human judge communicates with both a person and a computer through text messages or typing. If the judge cannot reliably tell which one is the machine and which one is the human, the computer is said to have passed the Turing Test. The idea is that real thinking can be shown through conversation.

 

10. What did Turing predict would happen by 2000? Did it happen in your opinion?

Turing predicted that by the year 2000, computers would be able to fool humans 30% of the time in a five-minute conversation. In my opinion, this didnt completely happen by 2000, but modern chatbots and AI like ChatGPT have come much closer to his prediction. They can hold realistic conversations, though they still lack real understanding or emotions.

12. Who was Eugene Goostman?

Eugene Goostman was a chatbot designed to sound like a 13-year-old boy from Ukraine. It became famous in 2014 for being one of the first programs to pass the Turing Test by convincing some judges that it was human during a conversation.
